About

Graeme Lithgow is an independent researcher with a focus on the intersection of cognitive control, information theory and network neuroscience. With a background in neurodivergence and human behaviour, Graeme investigates how specialised cognitive architectures shape our perception of the world and how information bottlenecks influence learning and decision‑making.

His work draws on interdisciplinary methods from psychology, psychiatry, artificial intelligence and computational neuroscience. The goal is to develop frameworks that better capture the unique strengths and challenges associated with neurodiverse minds while leveraging insights from information theory to design more equitable and efficient systems.

Research Focus

  • Cognitive control architectures in neurodivergence: understanding how executive functions adapt in individuals with autism and ADHD, and designing interventions that play to neurodiverse strengths.
  • Information bottleneck theory applications: exploring how information bottlenecks shape learning processes, memory formation and decision‑making in complex neural systems.
  • Network neuroscience frameworks: developing network‑level models to describe brain connectivity and identify emergent properties associated with specialised cognitive functions.
